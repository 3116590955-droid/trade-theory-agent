Abstract 
I consider the optimality of export subsidies in oligopolistic markets, when home 
and foreign firms have different costs and the social cost of public funds exceeds 
unity. Subsidies are optimal only for surprisingly 
low values of the social cost of 
public funds and, if subsidies are justified, 
they should be higher the more cost 
competitive 
are domestic firms. These results hold under both Cournot and Bertrand 
competition 
and in a two-period 
perfect equilibrium 
with learning 
by doing. The 
results suggest that recent arguments 
for export subsidies are more applicable 
to 
firms which possess a ‘comparative 
advantage in profit shifting’.

words:

6. Concluding 
remarks 
In summary, 
this paper has focused on two key asymmetries, 
between 
social and private costs and between home and foreign production costs, and 
has examined their implications for the sign and magnitude of the optimal 
export subsidy. The thrust of the paper’s findings is that, firstly, the optimal 
subsidy is likely to become negative at surprisingly low values of the social 
opportunity 
cost of funds; and, secondly, it is likely to be higher in absolute 
value the more cost-competitive 
is the home firm. These results hold in 
striking form in the simplest model considered, with Cournot behaviour and 
linear demands: in this case, the threshold value of the social opportunity 
cost of funds is only one and one-third, well within the empirically plausible 
range. However, 
with suitable qualifications, 
both results continue to hold 
under a variety of assumptions about the nature of consumer demand and 
the timing of decisions by firms and governments. 
A further 
contribution 
of the paper 
is to relate 
the argument 
for 
subsidising cost-competitive 
firms to the infant-industry 
argument, 
which 
suggests that firms with high costs in the present should be subsidised if they 
will benefit from learning by doing. These views were reconciled in Section 
4, where, drawing on Leahy (1992), I present what seems to be the first 
two-period, 
four-stage 
perfect-equilibrium 
model of public policy in the 
presence 
of learning by doing. It turns out that static cost competitiveness 
continues to justify a higher subsidy in this model. By contrast, a dynamic 
cost advantage,
