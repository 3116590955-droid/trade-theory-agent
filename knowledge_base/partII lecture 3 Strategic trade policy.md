Overview Strategic Trade Policy
We analyse trade policy under imperfect competition
Consequences of trade policy differ from perfect competition case; we investigate tariffs and quota
This opens up room for beneficial government intervention
Strategic trade policy analyses these possibilities; we look at Brander – Spencer and Eaton – Grossman
In practice, the possibilities for beneficial policy are limited; governments face many hidden traps and may do more harm than good

Market power and trade restrictions
The impact of trade restrictions depends on the type of competition on the domestic market
Quotas are more restrictive than tariffs; with tariffs firms are always confronted with the threat of foreign competition   (hence ‘tariffication’ preference for WTO)
Imperfect competition generally leads to sub-optimal outcomes; in principle leeway for government intervention
Governments trying to influence competitive position of domestic firms through trade policy = strategic trade policy

Strategic trade; Brander – Spencer model
Imagine ..
Two countries, America (A) and Britain (B), producing and exporting a good exclusively to a third country, China (C)
The good is only consumed in China, not in America & Britain
Firm A is the only producer in America and firm B in Britain
The firms use Cournot duopoly competition in China
Because of this: firm A's profits are a perfect welfare measure for America; similarly for firm B and Britain
What should the American government do?

[Slide 19]
Brander – Spencer model; quantity competition
The Brander – Spencer ‘optimal policy’ prescription:
Subsidize exporting firm; this provides a ‘credible threat’ to competitors by shifting the reaction curve; increases welfare
Note that the BS framework is very restrictive, e.g.
Cournot quantity competition only
Two firms exporting to a third market
No consumption in the producing countries
Informational requirements: the government must know everything (demand, supply, cost structure, competition) to determine optimal policy
Mistakes obviously lead to suboptimal outcome
See other objections to come ..

[Slide 20]
Brander – Spencer model ;  Political argument
Protection is a way of allocating resources in the economy, i.e. influencing production and distribution of welfare.
Markets are imperfect so there may be an economic argument for protection, but politicians are also imperfect, so the question is:
Which is more imperfect? Politically chosen import prices, or market chosen?
Powerful special interest groups often manage to get governments to put in policies that help few rich and harm many less rich consumers.

[Slide 21]
Strategic trade; Eaton – Grossman model
Eaton & Grossman use the same modelling structure as Brander & Spencer, with one twist
So: America and Britain each have one firm producing and exporting a good exclusively to China, the only consumer
This time the firms are involved in Bertrand price competition
Price competition makes the choice variables “strategic complements”; pB↑ raises marginal profitability of pA↑ (rather than “strategic substitutes” in Cournot quantity competition)
Again: what should the American government do?

[Slide 23]
The Eaton – Grossman ‘optimal policy’ prescription:
Levy an export tax; this provides a ‘credible threat’ to competitors by shifting the reaction curve; increases welfare
This is the exact opposite of the Brander – Spencer subsidy!   (in contrast to the BS subsidy, which firms tend to like, the government may not become popular using the EG tax policy)
Krugman therefore notes:
“So what Eaton and Grossman show is that replacing the Cournot with a Bertrand assumption reverses the policy recommendation. Given the shakiness of any characterization of oligopoly behaviour, this is not reassuring.”

[Slide 24]
Strategic trade policy – evaluation
Government involvement in strategic trade policy is a tricky business for many reasons. Some caveats
Optimal response hinges on details of market structure
Competition for resources; a subsidy benefits one sector at the expense of another: which sectors to favour?
Entry and exit; strategic trade hinges on supernormal profits; this does not work if firms enter the market in response to that
Retaliation; other governments may enter the strategic trade game; net results for players depend on many details
Informational requirement; to ‘do the right thing’ the government has to know virtually everything about virtually all markets, throughout the world. This info is not available and the government is not able to process it either

[Slide 25]
Concluding Remarks
Trade policy has a different effects with imperfect competition
Quotas are generally more restrictive than tariffs
Strategic trade policy may work under certain circumstances
But the policy prescriptions are not robust, require lots of information, depend on many details, and are subject to retaliation and other caveats
It is true that models of imperfect competition result in sub-optimal outcomes, but Romer (1994) already notes:
“.. the assertion that an equilibrium is not first-best Pareto optimal does not validate any arbitrary intervention. It just raises the stakes. Trade restrictions are a little bit harmful in the usual model of perfect markets. They are very harmful in the second-best world with fixed costs (i.e. imperfect competiton; CvM) described below.”
